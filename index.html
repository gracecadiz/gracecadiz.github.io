Dr. Grace Cadiz

Builder · Operator · Convener  ·  San Francisco, CA

I hold a doctorate in Human Rights Education. I built AI tools from scratch. I think those are related.

The through-line is institutional honesty: finding the gap between what an organization says it does and what actually happens, and building the systems, relationships, and teams to close it.

I believe proximity to the problem is the most underrated form of expertise. In every sector I have worked in, it is also the most consistently defunded.

Background

Classroom teacher. Nonprofit operations. Two Chief of Staff tours at high-growth tech companies, including one that scaled from 100 to 300+ employees at a $1B+ valuation. Then six years building the operational backbone of a global education program at the Atlassian Foundation: the team, the systems, the processes, the partnerships, the measurement infrastructure.

Four sectors. Same instinct: find the gap between what an institution says it does and what actually happens, and close it.

Where I am headed: building the infrastructure that helps institutions act at the speed and scale their problems demand. That includes how philanthropy deploys capital, how organizations learn from the communities closest to the problem, and how emerging technology gets governed by the people it affects most.

Exploring

  • Whether the organizations closest to the communities most affected by systemic problems have genuine decision-making power, or just a seat at the table. And what changes when you build for the former instead of settling for the latter.
  • What it actually takes to build trust-based philanthropy at institutional scale, not just as a philosophy but as an operational reality, with the systems, measurement, and accountability structures to sustain it.
  • How fast emerging technologies are reshaping the lives of young people across school, work, and identity, and whether the civic and philanthropic institutions meant to respond are designed for this moment or the last one.
  • Whether philanthropy and civic institutions are building for the long tail of catastrophic risk, in AI, in climate, in the compounding crises that follow both, or whether the incentive structures of most funding still reward short-term legibility over the slow, unglamorous work of preparedness.

Principles

On Work

Your systems are your values. Everything else is aspiration.

It is easy to say what you value. What is harder, and more revealing, is looking at what you have actually built. How you allocate resources, who gets heard when there is conflict, what you do when a relationship gets hard: these tell the truth about what you believe, regardless of what you say. Good intentions and poorly designed systems are not mutually exclusive. The structure is the evidence.

You cannot solve a problem you have not been close to.

Proximity is not just physical. It is relational. You have to be close enough to the people inside a problem for them to trust you with what is actually true, the unpolished version, the real constraints, the thing they would not say in a formal meeting. That is the information that makes a real solution possible. You cannot get it from a distance.

The goal is to do the thing. Not to perfect the plan for it.

It is easy to fall in love with a strategy and never actually launch it. But launching is not the finish line either. Start before you are ready. What happens next will teach you things the plan never could. Use that. The best version of anything is never the first one.

Get it right. Being right is a much smaller ambition.

The goal is the best outcome, not the vindication. If you have better information than I do, I want it, even if it means I was wrong. Most people protect the wrong answer because admitting error feels like losing. It never is. The wrong answer, held onto, always costs more than the admission would have.

Loyal to the purpose. Not to the structure built to serve it.

Build things from scratch and build them well. But stay clear on what the structure is actually for. A program, a team, a process that made sense at launch can outlive its usefulness entirely. Keeping it running because of the effort it took to build is a bad reason. It is also the most common one. When what you have built stops serving why you built it, the honest move is to change it.

On Life

Hold both maps: the world as it is, and the world as it could be.

Life is science fiction until it is not. The realism is for diagnosis: what is actually here, what the real constraints are, what is true right now. The imagination is for direction: what is worth building, what could exist, what the world might look like. Most people are good at one and use it for everything. The work is knowing which map the moment requires, and being able to pick it up and put it down.

Soft is not a category. It is a gap in analysis.

Trust, relationships, care: these are not the absence of rigor. They are where rigor is most needed and least applied. The things we dismiss as soft often determine whether everything else works. Getting them wrong is just as costly as getting strategy wrong. Usually more so.

If I am the smartest person in the room, I am in the wrong room.

I learn best when I am in over my head, when the people around me know things I do not, see things I have not, have done things I am still figuring out. Comfort is a signal worth questioning. The room where I am performing what I already know is the room where I stop growing. I try to stay in rooms that require something of me I have not given yet.

The body knows things the mind is still working out.

I am an academic by choice. I live in frameworks, analysis, ideas. But the mind needs language to work. The body does not. Which means the body often has the answer while the mind is still forming the question. When something matters, I write and I move. That is not a contradiction with being rigorous. It is part of how thinking actually works.
